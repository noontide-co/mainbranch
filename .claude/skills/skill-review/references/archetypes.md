# Archetype Catalog (Hughes 9)

The brief draft step picks an archetype to anchor positioning, paired imagery, and headline-formula choice. **Pick one for the offer; optionally name a second for what the audience is currently trapped in.**

The archetype names a *narrative shape*. It is NOT a template. The skill never writes "we are the wounded healer" on a sales page — it lets the shape inform tone, image pairing, and headline framing.

**Progressive disclosure rule:** load this index when drafting the brief. Operator picks; skill loads the per-archetype detail file lazily. Never load all 9 detail files at once.

## Picker heuristics

- **Coaches, therapists, teachers** — Wounded Healer. The credibility comes from "I went through it."
- **B2B SaaS that automates a chore** — Savior. The product arrives and the chore goes away.
- **Indie product against incumbents** — David & Goliath. The audience already feels small vs. Goliath; you give them an artifact to fight back with.
- **Contrarian positioning** — Dark Hero. "The industry doesn't want you to know this." Use sparingly; it ages fast.
- **Comeback story** — Broken Hero. The audience has tried before and failed. Selling them another shot.
- **Aspirational lifestyle** — usually the wrong frame. Pick one of the above and let aspiration be the side-effect.

## Mapping audience archetype → offer archetype

The brief schema separates `archetype` (your offer) from `audience_current_archetype` (what they're trapped in). The reframe is the offer.

| Audience trapped in | Offer should embody |
|---|---|
| Victim | Savior or Wounded Healer |
| Tragedy Mindset | David & Goliath or Wounded Healer |
| Broken Hero | Savior or another Broken Hero who made it |
| (none / curious) | Dark Hero or David & Goliath |
