# David & Goliath

Small, righteous, vastly outmatched — but armed with one specific advantage. The story is the slingshot.

## When to invoke

- Indie products against incumbents.
- Challenger brands.
- Tools built explicitly *against* a market default (Forgejo vs. GitHub, Beancount vs. QuickBooks, Cal.com vs. Calendly, Cloudflare Pages vs. Vercel).
- Operators who already feel small and want an artifact to fight back with.

Do **not** invoke when you're not actually small. If you're the incumbent, picking David is dishonest and the audience clocks it.

## Audience-current trap → reframe

The audience is in **Tragedy Mindset** ("the giants always win, what's the point") or experiencing fresh frustration with an incumbent. The reframe: "the giant is slow, indifferent, vulnerable. Here's the slingshot."

## Do-not-state list

- "We're better than [Goliath]."
- "Goliath is evil." (Let the user reach that. Naming it makes you the villain.)
- "Disruption."
- "Revolution."

## Paired-imagery template

- **Hero:** Goliath's surface (the bloated dashboard, the enterprise sales call, the multi-page invoice) + David's surface (one terminal, one file, one number). Side-by-side, no caption explaining.
- **Mechanism:** the specific advantage made tangible (a 10-line config file, a single price, a public roadmap) + the absence of Goliath's friction (no demo call, no contract, no per-seat).
- **Proof:** a user who switched, in their words, naming what they couldn't do before.

## Headline formulas that work

- *"[Goliath's product] is great. We do one thing they can't."*
- *"For people who've outgrown [Goliath] but can't switch yet."*
- *"The [product category] that doesn't [Goliath's worst sin]."*
- *"Run your [thing] in [unit] without [Goliath's friction]."*

## Voice anchor

- Specific, technical, confident-but-not-cocky.
- Names Goliath by name when honest. Doesn't smear.
- Owns the trade-offs (David is smaller; some things David can't do).

## Anti-patterns

- David imagery (slingshot, stones). Too literal.
- "We're not your father's [category]."
- Marketing-speak claims of disruption.
- Pretending Goliath has no strengths.
