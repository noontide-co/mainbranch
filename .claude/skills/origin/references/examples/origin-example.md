# Origin

## The Before (Ordinary World)

Bloomfield, NJ. Son of a police officer and homemaker. Italian-American family.

Taught myself HTML and JavaScript at 12. Four years of computer science in high school. Captain of football and track. Class president. Carnegie Mellon, then NJIT for Information Systems.

Planned to become a developer. Then a friend got me an interview for a QA internship at UPS in 2008. I took it. Stayed for 17 years.

## The Pains (What Pushed Me)

Tried consulting while working full-time with three kids. Meetings during work hours. Constant anxiety. More stress than it was worth.

Tried teaching QAs on Skool. The audience was broke — bootcamp grads who'd already spent thousands. Ceiling at $30/month. Couldn't make it sustainable.

Putting in tons of work and time and effort. Not enough to quit the job. Stuck in the middle.

## The Desires (What Pulled Me)

Freedom. Be my own boss.

Legacy. Leave something to my kids — a 6-year-old son and twin 3-year-old girls. Multi-generational wealth.

And underneath it all: the developer dream that never died. AI tools reignited it.

## The Spark (Call to Adventure)

No single dramatic moment. Just a gradual recognition:

I light up when I build something that helps people.

Consulting didn't fit. Teaching QAs didn't fit. Skooly was wandering.

Then I found Main Branch.

## The Leap (Crossing the Threshold)

Main Branch has everything I've been searching for:
- A leader I respect and want to learn from
- Philosophy that resonates (active reference, reconnection, ownership)
- Technical work that excites me (GitHub, AI, integrations)
- Scale — potential to help a LOT of people

I'm not a solo founder looking for a product. I'm a builder looking for a mission.

This is different. I've never seen anything like it.

Full commitment means shutting down the distractions and going all in. Building this very skill is part of that commitment.

## The Trials (Fears Faced)

The fear: being seen as "that guy" who keeps chasing shiny objects.

The reframe: This isn't a random pivot. It's convergence. Everything I've tried has led here.

The difference between shiny object syndrome and finding your thing is whether you know why this is different.

I know.

## The Wisdom (What I Bring)

- 17 years of QA — I think in edge cases, quality, systems
- Teaching experience — I've translated complex ideas before
- The "technical-enough" bridge — not pure dev, not non-technical, right in the middle
- The honest searcher — I know what doesn't work
- Builder energy — I light up when what I make helps people
- First principles contributor — I'm not just using Main Branch, I'm building it

## The Values (What I Won't Compromise)

- Build things that actually help people
- Quality matters (17 years of QA doesn't leave you)
- Persistence — winners never quit
- Honesty about what's working and what isn't

## The Vision (What Success Looks Like)

Freedom to work on what matters. Contributing to something bigger than a solo project. Leaving a legacy for my kids — not just money, but proof that you can find your thing if you keep searching.

Main Branch. All in.

## Notes on This Example

**What worked:**
- Started with Ordinary World (safe, factual territory)
- "The developer dream that never died" emerged naturally
- No dramatic epiphany — handled "gradual recognition" well
- Trench story is forward-looking (building this skill)
- Values emerged from the story, not direct questions

**Key discovery:**
> "I'm not a solo founder looking for a product. I'm a builder looking for a mission."

This insight reframed everything. The user wasn't failing at solo entrepreneurship — they were looking for the wrong thing.

**Interview duration:** ~20 minutes
