# Redemption

Bad guy goes to jail. Wrong gets righted. The arc completes.

Redemption is the most powerful archetype in the catalog and the most dangerous to use literally. Hughes' rule applies hardest here: **never describe the ending**. Redemption pages that promise the buyer's transformation invite the buyer to test the promise — and the moment the promise is tested, conviction collapses.

Use Redemption as the **narrative spine** for the operator's story (the founder's own arc, the company's own arc) — never as the offer's promise to the buyer. The buyer assembles their own redemption arc from the operator's. That's the conversion.

## When to invoke

- For founder origin stories that include a genuine fall and rise.
- For company narratives where the company itself was once part of the problem it now solves.
- As the narrative tone for "second attempt" offers (paired with Broken Hero for the audience).
- For categories where the audience needs to see that *this kind of change* is possible — not yours specifically.

Do **not** invoke when:
- There is no actual fall in the story (then Wounded Healer is the right archetype, not Redemption).
- The operator wants to promise the buyer a specific transformation. Redemption resists promises; if you want a promise, pick Savior.
- The arc isn't complete or recent. Redemption needs distance — a fall + rise that just happened reads as marketing.

## Audience-current trap → reframe

The Redemption-paired audience is usually a **Broken Hero** (they've fallen and are debating whether to try again) or in **Tragedy Mindset** (they've stopped believing change is possible). Watching the operator's redemption arc — described as scenes, not a thesis — gives the reader permission to imagine their own.

The reframe is structural: the page demonstrates that this kind of change happens, with specifics. The reader transfers the possibility onto themselves without prompting.

## The Hughes rule (mandatory)

> "I never ever ever talk about how the story ends because your brain already knows. I'm talking about this is the moment that this occurs."

For Redemption, this rule isn't optional. The page describes the moment of transition — the choice that started the rise, the tool that broke the loop, the conversation that flipped the operator's posture — without describing the destination.

The destination is the reader's job to imagine. They will. And because they imagined it, they will defend it.

## Do-not-state list

- "We'll make you [end state]." (Promising the ending invites failure-testing. Don't do it.)
- "Guaranteed transformation."
- Before/after sales-page headlines without specifics.
- "From [bad state] to [good state] in [time period]."
- "I went from [low] to [high]." (Describing the arc directly steals the reader's discovery.)
- "You can do this too." (Telegraphs the conclusion you want them to assemble themselves.)

## Paired-imagery template

Skip the cliché before-and-after. Redemption imagery sits **mid-arc**:

- **Mid-arc imagery 1:** the operator at the moment of decision (the empty studio after the bad year, the inbox at 4am with the resignation letter half-written, the journal open to a specific page) **paired with** the first artifact of the new direction (a single line of code, one file in a new folder, the email sent instead of deleted).
- **Mid-arc imagery 2:** the work in progress (an unfinished prototype, a half-renovated space, a draft with track-changes) **paired with** the same work nine months later, still in progress, slightly further along.
- **No "after" shot.** The page never shows the operator in a fully redeemed state. The reader assumes the redemption from the trajectory, not from a destination shot.

## Voice anchor

- Past-tense for the fall. Present-tense for the work. Future-tense never.
- Specific dates, specific objects, specific failures named.
- No reflection-as-content ("I learned so much"). Show the lesson via the work.
- No reframing of the fall as a gift ("the best thing that ever happened to me"). Reads as sales-page laundering.
- No promises made to the reader, even soft ones.

## Reframe target (mandatory)

Redemption is rarely the offer's primary archetype. Pair it with:

- **`archetype: wounded-healer`** — the operator already walked the arc and now guides others. Redemption is the texture of the operator's story; Wounded Healer is the offer's shape.
- **`archetype: broken-hero`** — the audience is mid-arc and trying again. Redemption is the proof that the second attempt is real; Broken Hero is what the buyer is.

A pure-Redemption page (no second archetype) almost always sounds like a memoir. Memoirs don't convert.

## Example brand applications

- **A founder coaching second-time founders**: the operator's first company failed publicly; the offer is mentorship for the second attempt. Pair `archetype: wounded-healer` with the operator's bio carrying Redemption tone.
- **A recovery-adjacent product** (sober-curious app, financial recovery service): the founder's own recovery is the arc; the product is the artifact. Pair with Wounded Healer.
- **A consultancy that left a bigger consultancy**: the operator was once part of the orthodoxy and watched it rot; the work now is the correction. Pair with Dark Hero.
- **A studio that rebuilt after a bad year**: paired with Broken Hero in the audience slot. The studio's own rebuild is mid-arc imagery; the audience reads themselves into it.

## Anti-patterns

- "From rock bottom to seven figures" copy. The reader's brain refunds this on contact.
- Phoenix-from-the-ashes imagery (literal phoenixes, sunrise-after-storm shots).
- Hustle rhetoric ("I worked 18-hour days for two years").
- Implying the fall was strategic in retrospect.
- Promising redemption *for the buyer*. The arc is the operator's; the buyer borrows possibility, not a guarantee.
- Skipping the mid-arc and showing only "the new chapter." Without the fall on the page, there's no Redemption — just a launch.

## Pairing prompts (for image + text generation)

Pair-prompts hold the moment-of-decision on one side and the first specific artifact on the other:

- *"An empty studio at end of day, dust in the late light, abandoned half-finished work on the desk."* + *"The same desk, six weeks later, the same dust, but a single laptop now open with a new file unsaved."*
- *"A journal open to a page with a sentence half-finished, the date 14 months ago."* + *"The same journal, current page, dated this week, the same handwriting but a different verb tense."*
- *"A whiteboard with a strategy that didn't work, photographed before erasure."* + *"The same whiteboard, blank, with a single small note in the corner — three words, no diagram."*

The text describes the date, the object, the specific failure. The reader's brain finishes the arc.
