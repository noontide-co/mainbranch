# Dark Hero

The anti-hero. Breaks the rules to do what's right. The industry's defaults are wrong; the operator says so on the record and behaves accordingly.

Dark Hero is **conviction made public**. It's the Wounded Healer's contrarian sibling — same earnestness, but pointing the friction outward at category orthodoxy instead of inward at personal pain. The credibility is in the operator's willingness to take the hit for breaking the rule.

## When to invoke

- Offers that break a category convention by design (no contracts, no demos, no per-seat pricing, public roadmap, public price, MIT-licensed where the category is closed).
- Operators with strong, defensible opinions the mainstream rejects.
- Founders whose backstory includes a public departure from the consensus path (worked at Goliath, watched the rot, left).
- Audiences who already feel the category default is wrong but don't have language for why.

Do **not** invoke for shock value. Dark Hero needs a real conviction underneath; without one it ages into edgelord posturing within months. If the operator can't articulate the specific wrong they're correcting, pick David & Goliath instead — that frame doesn't require the moral weight.

## Audience-current trap → reframe

The Dark Hero audience is usually in **Tragedy Mindset** about the category ("every CRM is bloated, that's just the industry") or in **Victim** mode about a specific incumbent ("Salesforce locks me in"). The reframe: "the rule is real, but breaking it works, and here's the artifact that proves it."

Dark Hero rarely names the audience's archetype directly — the demonstration carries the reframe. The page shows the broken rule + a working alternative; the reader's brain magnetizes them and assembles "the rule was the problem."

## Do-not-state list

- "The [industry] doesn't want you to know this." (Conspiracy-pitch tone; reader's manipulation-radar fires.)
- "Forget everything you know." (Empty.)
- "Disrupting the industry." (Marketing-speak; ages weekly.)
- "Big Tech is evil." / "[Goliath] is evil." (Let the user reach that. Naming it makes you the villain.)
- Direct insults aimed at named competitors.
- "We're the [contrarian thing]." (Telegraphs identity instead of demonstrating it.)

The Dark Hero shows the broken rule and the alternative side-by-side. The verdict belongs to the reader.

## Paired-imagery template

- **The convention being broken:** the contract (the multi-page MSA), the locked dashboard (the modal blocking export), the long sales process (the calendar with three "discovery call" entries), the gatekeeper (the "contact sales for pricing" page) **paired with** the alternative (the open repo, the public price on the homepage, the self-serve setup, the one-line config).
- **The operator visible in the friction-free state:** sitting at a desk with a single terminal, no headset, no calendar full of demos. Working the way the offer lets the buyer work.
- **Proof:** a user who switched, in their own words, naming the specific convention they walked away from. Not a testimonial about how great the product is — a testimonial about what they don't have to do anymore.

## Headline formulas that work

- *"The [thing] [industry] doesn't admit out loud."* (Followed by the specific thing, demonstrated.)
- *"We don't [convention]. Here's why."* — and the next sentence is a specific reason, not a value proposition.
- *"[Specific industry default]. We don't do that."* (Followed by what the operator does instead.)
- *"For people who've outgrown [convention] but can't say so at work yet."*

## Voice anchor

- Specific, technical, willing to name the rule being broken.
- Confident-but-not-cocky. The Dark Hero earns conviction by being right, not by being loud.
- Owns the trade-offs. The rule exists for a reason; the offer accepts the trade-off and explains it.
- Past-tense for the realisation ("we noticed that…"); present-tense for the alternative ("so we do this instead").

## Example brand applications

- **Forgejo** vs. closed-source Git forges: rule = "you ship a forge as a SaaS"; broken rule = self-hostable, AGPL, federation-first.
- **Beancount** vs. consumer accounting: rule = "users want a GUI"; broken rule = plain-text ledgers in version control.
- **Cal.com** vs. closed-source scheduling: rule = "scheduling is a SaaS"; broken rule = open source, self-hostable, API-first.
- **A landing-page builder** that ships pages as static HTML with no JS: rule = "modern sites are React apps"; broken rule = `<html>` files served from a CDN.
- **A consultancy** that posts every client deliverable to a public GitHub repo (with consent): rule = "deliverables are confidential"; broken rule = the work itself is the marketing.

## Anti-patterns

- Manifesto-style sales pages with no demonstration ("we believe..." paragraphs).
- Aggressive comparison tables that name and shame competitors.
- "Anti-establishment" copy without an actual establishment being challenged.
- Breaking a rule that doesn't matter to the buyer (rule-breaking as virtue signal, not as buyer benefit).
- Edgelord branding (skull imagery, "rebel" positioning, all-caps claims) — borrows the surface of conviction without the substance.
- Performing the contrarian frame while privately running the orthodox playbook.

## Pairing prompts (for image + text generation)

Pair-prompts that hold the convention on one side and the operator's alternative on the other:

- *"A 47-page MSA contract spread across a desk, highlighter marks. Cool office light."* + *"A single self-serve signup screen on a laptop, the same desk, warm morning light. The screen shows a price."*
- *"A calendar booking screen with three 'discovery call' slots over the next two weeks."* + *"A terminal window showing `npx <product> init`, completing in under a second."*
- *"A pricing page that says 'Contact sales for enterprise pricing' with a form."* + *"A pricing page that lists three tiers, all visible, with the prices in large numerals at the top."*
- *"A dashboard with an 'Export' button greyed out, a tooltip reading 'Available on Enterprise plan.'"* + *"A terminal command writing the same data to `users.csv` on the local disk."*

The text block names the convention. The image carries the alternative. The conclusion ("the rule was the problem") belongs to the reader.
