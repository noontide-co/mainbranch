# Tragedy Mindset

Nothing can change. Fate is sealed. This is just how it is.

Tragedy Mindset is **belief, not circumstance**. Where Victim is "the world did this to me," Tragedy Mindset is "the world is like this and always will be." The buyer has stopped trying. The offer's job is to interrupt the worldview, not to commiserate with it.

Like Victim, this archetype belongs in the brief schema's `audience_current_archetype` field — never in `archetype`. Position your offer in Tragedy Mindset and you're agreeing with the audience's resignation. Position the audience there and they reject the page on contact.

## When to invoke

- As `audience_current_archetype` when the buyer has tried before and has *settled into* the failure (not just experienced it once).
- For commodity-feeling categories where every option is the same and the buyer has stopped shopping (CRMs, accounting software, gym memberships).
- For audiences whose objection is "nothing works in this industry / for people like me."
- To name the worldview the offer's reframe interrupts.

Do **not** invoke for fresh-pain audiences (use Victim or Broken Hero instead). Tragedy Mindset is the petrified version — the buyer has accepted the trap as a feature of reality.

## Audience-current trap → reframe

The Tragedy Mindset reader believes change isn't available. The offer's job is to introduce a single specific anomaly — one fact, one detail, one demonstration — that doesn't fit the resigned worldview. The brain magnetizes the anomaly to the worldview and snaps the worldview open.

Reframe targets:

- **`archetype: david-goliath`** — "the giant is slow, indifferent, vulnerable, here's the slingshot." Best when the resignation is industry-wide.
- **`archetype: wounded-healer`** — "the belief was breakable; I broke it." Best when the resignation is identity-deep ("people like me don't…").
- **`archetype: dark-hero`** — "the rule is real, but breaking it works." Best when the resignation is rule-bound ("nobody does X in this industry").

## Do-not-state list

- "Don't be so negative." (Pep-talk; reads as dismissal of lived experience.)
- "Mindset is everything." (Cliché; ages instantly. Also: it isn't.)
- "Believe in yourself." (Sales-page noise.)
- "It doesn't have to be this way." (Telegraphs the punchline. Show the anomaly instead.)
- "Most people give up. Not you." (Flattery; the reader knows they did give up.)

## Paired-imagery template

The Tragedy Mindset visual is a **closed loop with one detail that doesn't fit**:

- **The closed-loop image:** the calendar full of the same week, the same desk in the same light, the same dashboard at 9am Monday for 47 weeks, the meeting that recurs forever, the inbox that fills as fast as it empties. **Paired with:** one frame where something is small but specifically different — a single number changed, a different file open on the second monitor, a window open that's never been open before.

The reader's eye finds the anomaly and the brain assembles "wait, what changed?" That micro-question is the conversion event. The page never names the change; the next block introduces the offer that produced it.

This is Hughes' "two pieces of information and there's no string between them, they make you put the string between the ideas" rule applied to resignation. The closed loop is the worldview; the anomaly is the slingshot.

## Headline formulas (use sparingly)

The Tragedy Mindset slot is mostly visual. When copy is needed, it should describe the closed loop without naming it:

- *"For the 6th [time period] in a row, [scene that has never changed]."*
- *"You've stopped expecting [thing the offer delivers]. We get it."* (Followed immediately by an unstated demonstration of the thing.)
- *"Most [audience] don't even try this anymore."* (Earns its right to exist only if the next block shows why some still do.)

## Voice anchor

- Past-imperfect tense for the loop ("was always like this," "never worked before").
- No "but" transitions ("but here's why this is different" telegraphs the shift). Let the next visual block carry the turn.
- Specific to the audience's exact loop. Generic "tired of feeling stuck" copy reinforces resignation by failing to name a real loop.

## Example brand applications

- **A B2B accounting tool** entering a market dominated by an incumbent: `audience_current_archetype: tragedy-mindset` ("every accounting tool is QuickBooks-shaped"); `archetype: david-goliath` (here's the one that isn't).
- **A second-attempt fitness program** for chronically gym-cycling adults: `audience_current_archetype: tragedy-mindset` ("nothing sticks for me, that's just my body"); `archetype: wounded-healer` (the operator's own loop and what broke it).
- **An indie SaaS positioning against a vendor-locked enterprise default**: `audience_current_archetype: tragedy-mindset` ("you have to use [Goliath]"); `archetype: dark-hero` (no, you don't, here's why we exist).

## Anti-patterns

- "Break free!" / "Unlock your potential!" (Pep-talk; closes the page.)
- "What if I told you..." (Conspiracy-pitch tone; reader smells manipulation.)
- Before-and-after testimonial pairs without specifics (the reader has seen 10,000 of these and the genre is dead).
- Naming the worldview directly ("you're stuck in a tragedy mindset"). The reader rejects the label.

## Pairing prompts (for image + text generation)

Pair-prompt the visual generator to produce the closed loop with one anomaly. The connecting line is missing on purpose:

- *"A weekly calendar view, the same Tuesday block highlighted in red across 47 weeks. Visualise as a single dense grid."* + *"The same calendar, but on the most recent Tuesday block, the red is replaced with a single line of green."*
- *"A standing desk with a monitor showing the same SaaS dashboard, identical morning light, identical coffee mug, identical state."* + *"The same desk, same light, same mug — but the dashboard shows a number the reader hasn't seen before."*
- *"An inbox at end-of-day Friday with 84 unread emails. Composed phone screen."* + *"The same inbox at end-of-day Friday, 84 unread, but a single email pinned at the top with a subject line that doesn't match the others."*

The text block that accompanies the pair describes the loop, names the date, refuses to name the anomaly. The reader does the assembly.
