# Wounded Healer

The person who suffered, healed, and now guides others through the same path. The credibility is in the pain itself; the offer is the path back.

## When to invoke

- Coaches, therapists, recovery practitioners, embodiment teachers.
- Founders whose origin story is the offer's mechanism (you built it because you needed it).
- Educators teaching what they wish they'd learned earlier.

Do **not** invoke when the founder hasn't actually walked the path. Wounded Healer is unforgiving of fakery; the audience can smell it.

## Audience-current trap → reframe

The audience is usually trapped in **Victim** or **Tragedy Mindset**. They believe nothing they've tried has worked, or that the problem is just who they are. The Wounded Healer reframe: "I was where you are. I found a way out. Here it is."

## Do-not-state list

These conclusions belong to the reader. The skill does NOT write them as headlines.

- "I understand what you're going through." (Show it via specific pain phrases, don't claim it.)
- "This will change your life." (Let the testimonials carry that weight.)
- "I was just like you." (Probably weren't. Stay specific to your story.)
- "Most coaches don't get it. I do." (Comparison-as-credibility ages fast.)

## Paired-imagery template

Each visual block is a pair (per the Hughes paired-imagery rule):

- **Hero:** an artifact of the work (a journal, a worn pair of running shoes, a guitar, a kitchen counter at 3am) + a moment of recovery (the same artifact in a different light, the room with the operator visible). The reader's eye snaps from before-state to after-state.
- **Mechanism:** a tool of the practice (whiteboard with a framework, hands holding a worn book) + a person at the practice. No staged stock photography.
- **Testimonial:** a single object the testifier mentioned by name in their words + the testifier's own photo, no studio lighting.

## Headline formulas that work

- *"I spent X years [in the trap]. Here's what I'd tell my younger self."*
- *"[Specific pain phrase the audience uses verbatim]. I know — I was there. Here's what changed it."*
- *"For the [audience descriptor] who's tried [common attempts] and is still [stuck state]."*

## Voice anchor

- First person, past tense for the suffering, present tense for the offer.
- Names specific tools, specific moments, specific failures.
- Avoids "transformation," "journey," "breakthrough" — the words have been laundered by every coach in the algorithm.

## Anti-patterns

- Stock-photo hands cradling a candle.
- "Discover the secret to..."
- Long sales-page redemption arcs that promise a final state.
