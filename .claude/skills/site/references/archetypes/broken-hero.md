# Broken Hero

A hero who has fallen and must rise again. The story is not "I rose"; it's "I fell, and I'm rising."

## When to invoke

- Comeback stories. Founders on second or third attempts.
- Audiences who have tried before and failed (weight loss, addiction recovery, business launches, music careers).
- Offers framed as "the right way this time."

Do **not** invoke when there is no fall in the story. Broken Hero requires the breakage; without it the archetype is just Wounded Healer.

## Audience-current trap → reframe

The audience is usually a **Broken Hero** themselves — they've tried, failed, and are wary of trying again. The reframe is recognition: "I've been there. The mistake was X. Here's a better attempt."

## Do-not-state list

- "Don't give up!" (Pep-talk language; reads as condescension.)
- "I never gave up." (Suggests they should match your willpower.)
- "If I can do it, anyone can." (False equivalence.)
- "It's never too late." (Cliche.)

## Paired-imagery template

- **Hero:** a moment of failure (the closed shop, the failed launch screenshot, the empty room) + the rebuild (the same shop with renovations underway, the new product in beta).
- **Mechanism:** the specific lesson learned (a notebook page with the diagnosis, a new framework on a whiteboard) + the lesson applied (the operator using the framework on the new attempt).
- **Proof:** a user who'd already failed once with a similar approach, now succeeding with the offer.

## Headline formulas that work

- *"I tried [X]. It failed. Here's what I'd do differently."*
- *"For founders who've already shipped one [thing that didn't work]."*
- *"Built for the second attempt, not the first."*

## Voice anchor

- Past-tense for the failure, present-tense for the rebuild.
- Specific failures, not "things didn't work out."
- No "I learned so much." Show the lesson.

## Anti-patterns

- "Phoenix from the ashes" imagery.
- "Hustle" rhetoric.
- Implying the audience's previous attempts were their fault.
