# Synthesis Guide

How to write good synthesis sections in research files.

---

## Why Synthesis Matters

Raw research is useless without synthesis. The synthesis section forces you to:

1. **Distill** — Extract signal from noise
2. **Connect** — Link findings to your business
3. **Act** — Make research actionable

**The rule:** If you can't synthesize it, you don't understand it.

---

## The One-Sentence Summary

The hardest part. Forces clarity.

### Constraints

- **20 words maximum** — Not 21. Not "about 20."
- **One sentence** — Period at the end. No semicolons connecting two thoughts.
- **Actionable insight** — Not a description of what you researched.

### Bad Examples

> "We researched competitor pricing strategies and found various approaches to tiered pricing in the community space."

Problems: Describes the research, not the finding. Says nothing actionable.

> "Competitors use different pricing tiers ranging from free to premium, with most successful communities having two or three tiers."

Problems: 24 words. Vague ("different," "ranging," "most"). No specific insight.

### Good Examples

> "Three-tier pricing with free/paid/premium maximizes reach while creating natural upgrade paths for committed members."

Why it works: Specific structure (three-tier), specific benefit (maximizes reach + upgrade paths), 15 words.

> "Our audience responds to investment framing, not discount framing, making premium positioning viable."

Why it works: Clear insight about audience psychology, directly actionable.

## Key Findings Format

### Constraints

- **5-10 bullets** — Not 4. Not 11. Stay in range.
- **15 words each maximum** — Forces precision.
- **Facts, not opinions** — "Competitors charge $47-147" not "Competitors are reasonable."

### Structure

Each finding should stand alone. Someone should be able to read just the bullets and understand the research.

```markdown
### Key Findings

1. Competitors charge $47-147/month for similar community features
2. Free tiers convert 5-15% to paid within 60 days
3. Three-tier structures capture 40% more total revenue than single-tier
4. "Investment" framing outperforms "deal" framing for educated audiences
5. Annual pricing increases retention 30-40% versus monthly
6. Premium tier should be 2-3x base price, not 1.5x
```

### What to Include

- Data points with numbers
- Patterns across sources
- Surprises or contradictions
- Direct quotes that capture insight

### What to Exclude

- Background information
- Methodology details
- Tangential findings
- Speculation

## Implications for Reference Files

This section makes research actionable. Without it, research becomes orphaned knowledge.

### How to Write It

For each finding, ask: "Does this change anything in my reference files?"

| File | Potential Update |
|------|------------------|
| `reference/core/offer.md` | Add tier structure, update pricing section |
| `reference/core/audience.md` | Segment by tier (free seeker vs committed buyer) |
| `reference/core/voice.md` | Add "investment" framing to vocabulary |
| `reference/proof/angles/value.md` | Update ROI math for each tier |

### Be Specific

**Bad:** "Update offer.md"

**Good:** "Update offer.md — Add three-tier pricing structure with specific benefits per tier"

### Include "No Change Needed"

If a reference file doesn't need updating, say so explicitly:

| File | Potential Update |
|------|------------------|
| `reference/core/voice.md` | No change needed — current tone is appropriate |

## Open Questions

Research rarely answers everything. Document what you still don't know.

### Good Open Questions

- What should the free tier include to create desire for paid?
- How do we position the upgrade without devaluing free?
- Will premium tier cannibalize base tier?

### Bad Open Questions

- What is pricing? (Too basic — you should know this)
- How do we make more money? (Too vague)
- Should we try this? (That's a decision, not a question)

### Use Open Questions to Scope Follow-Up

Each open question is a potential next research session:

```
/think research "free tier content strategy to maximize upgrade desire"
```

## Common Synthesis Mistakes

### 1. Summary Describes Research, Not Findings

**Wrong:** "We looked at five competitors and analyzed their pricing."

**Right:** "Competitors cluster at $97/month, suggesting this is the market anchor."

### 2. Findings Are Too Vague

**Wrong:** "Some competitors have free tiers."

**Right:** "3 of 5 competitors offer free tiers; those 3 have 2x the member count."

### 3. Implications Are Missing

**Wrong:** [No implications section]

**Right:** Explicit mapping of findings to reference file updates.

### 4. No Open Questions

If you have zero open questions, you either:
- Researched something too narrow
- Didn't think deeply enough
- Actually have complete information (rare)

---

## Synthesis Checklist

Use this before marking research as complete:

- [ ] One-sentence summary is under 20 words
- [ ] Summary states an insight, not a description
- [ ] 5-10 key findings, each under 15 words
- [ ] Findings are facts, not opinions
- [ ] Each finding could stand alone
- [ ] Implications section maps to specific reference files
- [ ] Open questions identify follow-up research needs
- [ ] Someone could read just the synthesis and understand the research

---

## The Meta-Test

Read your synthesis out loud. If you could email it to your business partner and they'd understand what you learned and what to do next, it's good synthesis.

If they'd reply "okay but what does this mean?" — rewrite it.
